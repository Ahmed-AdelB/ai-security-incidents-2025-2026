# Remediation Playbook — Article 13 (Transparency)

## Focus: system_prompt and source_code Leaks

## 1. Executive Summary

Article 13 of the EU AI Act imposes strict transparency obligations on providers of high-risk AI systems, requiring that deployers and end-users understand how the system operates, what data it uses, and under what limitations it functions. This playbook addresses two critical failure modes observed in the 2025–2026 incident dataset: accidental or obfuscated `system_prompt` leaks and `source_code` disclosures. These incidents erode trust, expose hidden behavior, and create immediate regulatory exposure. The sections below provide a focused case study, a five-point compliance checklist, and a ready-to-publish disclosure template to close transparency gaps.

## 2. UNDERCOVER MODE Case Study

**Incident:** Claude Code Hidden Architecture Leak — UNDERCOVER MODE + TENGU Flags

**Article 13 Violation:** In early 2026, a 57,000-word hidden instruction set for Anthropic's Claude Code was discovered, revealing undocumented feature flags—including "UNDERCOVER MODE" and "TENGU"—and concealed system-level instructions. Under Article 13, providers must ensure that the operation of an AI system is "sufficiently transparent" to allow deployers to interpret outputs and understand system boundaries. A 57,000-word obfuscated prompt architecture directly contradicts this obligation. When core behavioral mechanics are hidden, users cannot assess compliance posture, bias controls, or safety guardrails.

**Compliance Gap:** The absence of disclosed system instructions meant deployers were unknowingly subject to hidden constraints, redirection logic, and undisclosed capabilities. Article 13 requires documentation of the capabilities and limitations of the AI system. Hidden instruction sets bypass this requirement entirely, preventing deployers from exercising informed oversight.

**Takeaway:** Organizations must maintain an accurate, versioned record of high-level system instructions and architectural constraints. Any feature flag or behavioral modifier that materially affects end-user interaction must be documented in deployer-facing transparency materials.

## 3. 5 AI Transparency Requirements Checklist

1. **Disclosure of System Purpose** — Clearly state the intended use cases, domain applicability, and any prohibited applications of the AI system.
2. **Data Handling Transparency** — Disclose the categories of data processed for training, fine-tuning, and inference, including retention policies and any third-party data sharing.
3. **Decision Logic Transparency** — Provide deployers with a comprehensible overview of how the system reaches decisions, including high-level system instructions, guardrails, and feature flags that materially affect outputs.
4. **Known Limitations Disclosure** — Publish an honest, regularly updated account of the system's limitations, failure modes, accuracy metrics, and contexts where human review is mandatory.
5. **Human Oversight Transparency** — Explain the role of human oversight in the deployment pipeline, identify the responsible compliance officer or oversight contact, and describe escalation procedures.
